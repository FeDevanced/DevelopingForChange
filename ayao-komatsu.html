Ayao Komatsu


There are team principals who arrived at the top table through politics, through personality, through being in the right room at the right time. Ayao Komatsu got here by knowing more about the car than almost anyone else in the paddock, and then proving he could do everything else too.

Born in Tokyo, Komatsu grew up watching Formula 1 broadcast free-to-air across Japan, the kind of Saturday night television that quietly rewires a teenager's sense of what's possible. His defining memory of the sport came early: the 1989 Japanese Grand Prix, Senna and Prost, one of the most controversial moments in the history of the championship, witnessed from his home country. It left a mark.

At eighteen, he moved to the United Kingdom on his own to pursue it properly, enrolling at Loughborough University where he earned a degree in Automotive Engineering before going further, completing a PhD in Vehicle Dynamics and Control. It wasn't a shortcut. It was a foundation.

The Long Way Up

His career in F1 started in 2003 at British American Racing, working as a tyre engineer. Unglamorous, technical, essential. From there he moved to Renault in 2006, cutting his teeth as a performance engineer before earning his place on the race team. By 2012 he was Romain Grosjean's race engineer at what was by then the Lotus F1 Team, a partnership that became one of the more quietly successful driver-engineer relationships of that era, producing nine podiums across two seasons and real fights for race wins in 2013.

He was promoted to Chief Race Engineer in 2014, and when Haas arrived on the grid in 2016, Komatsu came with them, helping build the engineering operation of a brand new team from the ground up. He's been here ever since.

In January 2024, Gene Haas gave him the job.

Engineering at the Top

What makes Komatsu different is exactly what it sounds like. He doesn't lead from the media room or the hospitality suite. His instinct is the data, the process, the decision under pressure. When he took over, his stated priority was clear: empower the people around him and build structures that actually work. Results followed. In his first season as Team Principal, Haas finished seventh in the Constructors' Championship, their best result since 2018

He'll tell you the job is harder than it looks from the outside, because one minute you're in a technical debrief and the next you're thinking commercially, then strategically, then you're on the pitwall with a call to make in under three seconds. The engineering brain helps. So does having spent over two decades inside the sport earning the trust of everyone around him.

What He Stands For

Komatsu doesn't do noise. He doesn't court controversy, doesn't play the media game, doesn't perform for the cameras. What you get instead is substance, a leader who believes the fastest way to make the car quicker is to make the people around it better. His focus since taking charge has been on culture as much as performance: bringing in the right people, giving them the room to do their best work, and holding the whole thing together under pressure.

That approach is why TGR Haas is building toward something. Not just chasing points, but building a team that can consistently fight for them. Komatsu has been here since day one. He knows what this team is capable of, and he's not finished yet.
