Andrea De Zordo

Formula 1 cars don't build themselves. Behind every lap time, every aerodynamic decision, every chassis that passes homologation and makes it onto the grid, there's a technical director who made a thousand calls that nobody outside the garage ever hears about. At TGR Haas F1 Team, that person is Andrea De Zordo.

Born in Faenza, an Italian town with motorsport running so deep through its identity that it's practically in the water. De Zordo's path into F1 followed the kind of trajectory you'd expect from someone who grew up that close to the sport. He studied mechanical engineering at the University of Bologna, walked into his first job in motorsport with Minardi in 2001 as a stress engineer, and never looked back.

Built Across the Best Garages in the Paddock

What De Zordo brings to this team is rare: a career that spans almost the full width of the grid. From Minardi he moved to McLaren, spending five years in Woking as a senior design engineer. Then came Ferrari, first as Head of Mechanical Design, then as Deputy Chief Designer, where he spent years working on some of the most scrutinised machinery in the sport.

In 2021, he arrived at Haas as Chief Designer, part of the technical transfer that came with the team's deepening relationship with Maranello. He spent three years in that role, getting to know the operation inside out, before being promoted to Technical Director at the start of 2024.

He's one of the most experienced technical minds on the grid. And he's spent the last several years putting that experience to work on this car, for this team.

The Man Who Built the VF-26

The 2026 season represents the biggest regulation overhaul Formula 1 has seen in years, new aerodynamics, new power unit architecture, a near-even split between combustion and electrical power. Every team on the grid faces the same unknowns. What separates them is how early and how seriously they started solving the problem.

De Zordo started early. Concept work on the VF-26 began in the second half of 2024, quietly, with a small group working in parallel to the race programme. Resources shifted gradually across the season, fully transitioning after the summer break, with a skeleton crew still refining the 2025 car while the rest of the team pushed toward a completely new machine.

Getting two programmes across the line simultaneously, without losing ground on either, is exactly the kind of problem a technical director earns their reputation on. De Zordo managed it, and the car that rolled out in Barcelona in January 2026 was the result.

How He Leads

De Zordo doesn't separate technical performance from the people who deliver it. When he talks about what the design office achieved building the VF-26, he doesn't reach for statistics or timelines first — he talks about the team working into the middle of the night, not because anyone told them to, but because they wanted to. That, to him, is the real milestone.

It's a mindset that fits perfectly inside what Ayao Komatsu is building at the top of this team. Two technical leaders, both deeply invested in the detail, both focused on making the people around them better. The results speak for themselves.

We're not here just to participate. We're here to challenge.
